A coaching tool for real change

Immunity to Change

Most of us have tried to change something that genuinely matters to us. We know what we want. We know, roughly, what we should do. And we still do not do it.

This is not a failure of will. It is a system at work.

The Immunity to Change framework, developed by Robert Kegan and Lisa Laskow Lahey at Harvard, reveals the hidden structure beneath the surface of our self-defeating behaviour. Underneath every stuck place is a competing commitment we do not know we have, and beneath that, a big assumption we have never tested.

This session will take you through that structure, one stage at a time. You will build your own map. You will design one small experiment. And you will leave with something more useful than advice: clarity about what is actually going on.

The session takes most people between 40 and 60 minutes. Your answers are saved automatically as you go, so you can leave and return at any time. Nothing is shared or stored outside your browser.

A note before you begin

This tool is a structured reflection experience, not a substitute for therapy or professional mental health support. If difficult emotions come up as you work through it, please take a break and reach out to someone you trust. The Economist's Employee Assistance Programme is available if you need it.

Stage 1 of 5 · Improvement Goal

What do you genuinely want to change?

Think about something you genuinely want to change or improve. Not something you feel you should do, and not a task to complete. Something that actually matters to you: in your work, your relationships, your leadership, or the way you show up.

The more honest and specific you are here, the more useful the rest of the session will be.

Wheel of Life
Score these eight areas of your life from 1 (low) to 10 (high): Career, Relationships, Health, Finance, Personal Growth, Fun, Environment, Purpose. Where is the gap between where you are and where you want to be? That gap often points toward a meaningful goal.
Values Sort
Name your three most important values right now (examples: honesty, courage, connection, impact, calm, creativity). Ask yourself: which of those values is not being honoured in the way I am living or working? A goal that connects to a core value tends to be the real one.
Future Self Letter
Imagine it is two years from now and something significant has shifted. Write the first sentence of a letter from that future version of yourself: "Something that changed was..." What does that sentence want to say?
The Aliveness Test
Write down two or three things you are considering as your goal. Read each one aloud. Notice the difference in how your body responds. Does one feel more energising, more alive, more true? The goal that has a charge to it is usually the right place to work.

Read what you have just written. Does it feel honest? Is there a more specific or more courageous version waiting underneath? Take a moment. You can revise it before moving on.

Stage 2 of 5 · Behaviours

What are you doing that works against this?

Your goal

Now comes honest observation. What are you doing, or not doing, that actively works against the goal you just named? These are behaviours, not character flaws. They are data.

Include things you do too much of, things you avoid, and things you do instead of what would actually move you forward.

Fly on the Wall
Imagine a small camera has been following you this past week. It has recorded everything you actually did, not what you intended to do. What would it have captured that works against your goal? List what it saw without judging it. Just observe.
Time Audit
Think about where your time and energy actually went last week. What did you spend most time on? Now compare that to what would genuinely move your goal forward. What is the gap? What fills the space that your goal could occupy?
The "Instead" List
Complete this sentence as many times as you can: "Instead of [doing the thing that would serve my goal], I..." Often our patterns are most visible when we name what we do in their place.

Notice how it feels to write this. Guilt or shame? Curiosity? Neither is wrong. These behaviours have been doing a job. The next stage starts to reveal what that job is.

Stage 3 of 5 · Competing Commitments

What are you also committed to?

Your goal

This is the heart of the work. Here is what we know so far: you have a goal you care about, and you have behaviours that undermine it. The question is not "why don't you just stop those behaviours?" The question is: what are they protecting?

Start by imagining that you commit fully to your goal, right now. You change the behaviours. You do the thing. What worries come up? What feels risky or unsafe about that?

The Worry Cascade
Take one worry and ask: "And if that happened, what would be the worst part?" Then ask it again. And again. You are looking for the thing that feels genuinely threatening, not just uncomfortable. That is where the real commitment lives.
The "Yes, and" Move
Complete this: "I want to [my goal], AND I also want..." List as many "ands" as you can without filtering. Often the competing commitment is hiding in one of them. Look for the one that creates the most tension with your goal.
Whose Voice Is That?
When you imagine committing fully to your goal, whose face comes to mind? Whose reaction do you find yourself anticipating or managing? Often our worries are less about us and more about how we will be seen. Notice if a particular person or group appears.

Now look at those worries. Each one contains a signal about something you are also committed to protecting. These are not failures. They are competing commitments: real, legitimate, often long-held.

For each worry, try completing this: "Beneath this worry, I notice I am also committed to..." You might find you are committed to being seen as competent, to not disrupting relationships, to staying safe from a particular kind of judgment. Name them as clearly as you can.

Read these back. Do any of them surprise you? Which one has the most charge, the feeling of "yes, that is the one"? That is probably the most important. Carry it with you into the next stage.

Stage 4 of 5 · Big Assumptions

What must be true for this to make sense?

Your competing commitments

Your competing commitments do not arise from nowhere. Underneath each one is a belief, something that feels so obviously true that it does not feel like a belief at all. It feels like reality.

These are your Big Assumptions. They are often old. They were probably formed in response to real experiences. They have been doing useful work for a long time. But they may be running on outdated information.

For each competing commitment you named, try completing this: "I assume that if I let go of this commitment... then..." What is the consequence that feels inevitable?

Timeline Trace
Think about your big assumption. How old were you when this first felt true? What was happening then? You do not need to analyse it, just notice. Often the assumption was a very reasonable response to something that happened a long time ago, in a very different context.
Evidence Audit
Ask yourself: what evidence do I have that this assumption is true? Then ask: what evidence exists that it might not always be true, or might not be true in every case? You are not trying to disprove the assumption. You are just checking whether it has been tested recently.
The Scientist Stance
A scientist does not believe or disbelieve a hypothesis: they design a test. Try saying this to yourself: "I am treating this assumption as a hypothesis, not a fact. What would I need to observe to know whether it is accurate?" Notice how this changes your relationship to it.

How long have you held this assumption? Where might it have come from? You do not need to answer those questions now. Simply notice that this is an assumption, not a certainty. That is already significant.

Stage 5 of 5 · Experiment

What small experiment will you run?

Your big assumption

The way to change a Big Assumption is not to believe it or disbelieve it. It is to test it. Carefully. Safely. With curiosity rather than a need to prove anything.

A good experiment is small and low-risk. It creates a situation where your assumption will either be confirmed or contradicted by real data, not by argument. It is something you can do in the next one to two weeks.

You are not trying to change yet. You are trying to learn something.

Minimum Viable Experiment
Ask: what is the smallest possible action that would still give me real information about my assumption? Not a gesture, something that actually tests the belief. Small enough that failure would not be devastating. Real enough that success would mean something.
WOOP
Wish: what is the outcome you want from this experiment? Outcome: what would that look like in practice? Obstacle: what might get in the way (inner or outer)? Plan: when [obstacle occurs], I will [specific action]. WOOP turns good intentions into if-then commitments.
The Pre-mortem
Imagine the experiment has happened and it did not go as hoped. What went wrong? Walk backwards from that imagined failure. Now design around those risks before you start. This is not pessimism, it is useful preparation.
The Observation Frame
After you run the experiment, what will you look for? Name in advance the two or three things you will notice. How did others respond? How did you feel? What happened that you did not expect? Deciding what to observe before the experiment makes the data more useful.

Sharing an experiment with someone you trust is one of the most reliable ways to follow through. It also creates someone to reflect with afterwards.
